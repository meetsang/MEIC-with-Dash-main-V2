How to Read This Chart
  • Net GEX shows dealer delta hedging per 1-point move. Positive net GEX means dealers are net long gamma — they sell into rallies and buy dips, dampening volatility.
  • Green bars (positive GEX) = call-heavy strikes where dealer hedging creates upward pressure/resistance as price approaches.
  • Red bars (negative GEX) = put-heavy strikes where dealer hedging amplifies downward moves.
  • The cumulative line (purple) tracks running net GEX. Where it crosses zero is the gamma flip — the pivot between dampening and amplifying regimes.
  • Spot line (dashed) marks current price relative to walls and flip.
Trading Implications
  • Positive gamma regime (net GEX > 0): Favor range-bound strategies — iron condors, mean-reversion scalps. Expect pinning near high-OI strikes. Breakouts may fade.
  • Negative gamma regime (net GEX < 0): Trend moves can accelerate as dealers hedge in the same direction. Wider stops, smaller size, or sit out until regime shifts.
  • Call wall above spot acts as resistance; put wall below acts as support. Price often gravitates between them in positive gamma.
  • Approaching gamma flip from below: volatility may increase as you enter negative gamma territory.
  • 0DTE caveat: OI is an overnight snapshot; gamma explodes near ATM as expiry approaches. Recalculate every 15–30 min on expiry day.

GEX assumes dealers are net short customer options. This breaks down around major events or unusual positioning.
